### GRAT Analysis

A Grantor Retained Annuity Trust (GRAT) is an irrevocable trust designed to minimize estate taxes by freezing the value of transferred assets for transfer-tax purposes. The grantor transfers assets into the trust and retains the right to receive fixed annuity payments for a specified term. At the end of the term, any remaining assets pass to the beneficiaries, typically the grantor's children, without further gift tax implications [S001].

Shorter rolling GRATs could mitigate mortality risk by resetting the term, while longer terms increase the risk of estate inclusion.

#### Annuity Payment Structure

The annuity payments may increase by up to 20% annually, providing some flexibility in structuring payments [S002]. If the trust's returns fail to exceed the hurdle rate, the remainder passing to beneficiaries may be reduced or eliminated.

#### Tax Treatment

Under IRC §671, the GRAT is treated as a grantor trust for income tax purposes, meaning the grantor pays taxes on the trust's income, preserving the trust's corpus for beneficiaries [S002]. This structure allows for efficient wealth transfer while minimizing immediate tax liabilities.

#### Irrevocability and Administrative Complexity

The transfer to a GRAT is irrevocable, permanently removing the assets from the grantor's control. This lack of flexibility is a trade-off for the potential estate tax savings. The GRAT involves administrative complexity, including annual valuations and compliance with IRS regulations [S002].

The irrevocable nature and administrative requirements should be carefully considered in light of the client's liquidity needs and philanthropic flexibility.

## CRAT Analysis
