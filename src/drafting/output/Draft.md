## 2. Executive Summary

This report analyses two estate-planning strategies for a 62-year-old married client who realised a $16 million liquidity event from the sale of an advertising agency in 2015. The applicable federal estate tax rate is 40.00% and the Section 7520 hurdle rate used for present-value calculations is 196 bps (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.).

### Key Findings

A Grantor Retained Annuity Trust (GRAT) would save an estimated $1,482,050.71 in estate taxes while transferring wealth outside the taxable estate (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.). A Charitable Remainder Annuity Trust (CRAT) would produce estate tax savings of $3,048,289.06 and reduce the taxable estate to $8,379,277.36 (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.).

The two vehicles serve fundamentally different planning objectives. The GRAT prioritises wealth transfer to the next generation, while the CRAT prioritises charitable giving with a lifetime income stream to the grantor. The following sections detail each strategy's mechanics, projected outcomes, and trade-offs.

### Report Scope

This analysis is limited to the federal estate, gift, and income tax implications of the two trust structures. State-level taxes, alternative planning vehicles (e.g., dynasty trusts, family limited partnerships), and post-implementation investment management are outside the scope of this report (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.).

## 4. Grantor Retained Annuity Trust (GRAT)

### 4.1 Purpose and Structure

A Grantor Retained Annuity Trust is established under IRC Section 2702, which requires the grantor to retain a qualified annuity interest for a fixed term (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.). The trust is funded with $16,000,000.00 from the client's liquidity event proceeds. Under the applicable regulations, the present value of the retained annuity interest is determined using IRS valuation tables and the Section 7520 rate in effect at the time of the transfer (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.).

The GRAT is designed as an irrevocable trust that pays a fixed annuity to the grantor for a specified number of years. At the end of the trust term, any remaining assets pass to the designated beneficiaries — in this case, the client's two adult children — free of additional gift or estate tax (S004, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/1.664-2, n.d.).

### 4.2 Key Mechanics

The GRAT has a term of 10 years with an annual annuity payment of $1,777,498.67 returned to the grantor each year (S004, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/1.664-2, n.d.). Over the full term the total annuity payments equal $17,774,986.70.

The annuity is structured to approximate a "zeroed-out" GRAT where the present value of the annuity stream roughly equals the initial corpus, resulting in a taxable gift of $0.00 (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.). This minimises the gift tax cost of the transfer while allowing any growth above the Section 7520 hurdle rate to pass tax-free to beneficiaries (S004, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/1.664-2, n.d.).

### 4.3 Tax Treatment

The GRAT is a grantor trust for income tax purposes under IRC Section 671, meaning the grantor pays income tax on trust earnings during the term (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.). This is actually advantageous because it allows the trust to grow without the drag of income taxes, effectively providing a tax-free gift to the remainder beneficiaries.

For estate and gift tax purposes, the taxable gift equals the difference between the initial corpus and the present value of the retained annuity. At a Section 7520 rate of 196 bps, the taxable gift is $0.00 (S004, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/1.664-2, n.d.).

### 4.4 Advantages

- Wealth transfer efficiency: Assuming a growth rate of 5.00% in excess of the 196 bps hurdle rate, the projected remainder passing to children is $3,705,126.78 (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.)
- Low gift tax exposure: The zeroed-out structure minimises the taxable gift to $0.00 (S004, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/1.664-2, n.d.)
- Estate tax savings: The resulting taxable estate is reduced to $12,294,873.22, saving $1,482,050.71 in estate taxes (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.)
- Grantor trust status: Income taxes paid by the grantor further reduce the estate (S004, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/1.664-2, n.d.)

### 4.5 Risks and Limitations

- If the grantor does not survive the 10-year term, the full trust corpus is included in the gross estate under IRC Section 2033 (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.)
- If trust assets underperform the 196 bps hurdle rate, the remainder to beneficiaries will be reduced or eliminated (S004, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/1.664-2, n.d.)
- Legislative risk: Congress has periodically considered imposing minimum GRAT terms or eliminating zeroed-out GRATs (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.)
- The GRAT does not generate a charitable deduction; clients with strong philanthropic objectives may need a complementary vehicle (S004, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/1.664-2, n.d.)

## 5. Charitable Remainder Annuity Trust (CRAT)

### 5.1 Purpose and Structure

A Charitable Remainder Annuity Trust is governed by IRC Section 664, which requires a fixed annuity payout to the income beneficiary for a defined period, with the remainder passing to a qualified charity (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.). According to the regulations, "ch contributions does not exceed 50 percent of the taxpayer’s contribution base for the taxable year. (B) Other contributionsAny charitable contribution other than a charitable" (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.).

The CRAT provides the grantor with a reliable income stream while achieving estate tax reduction and fulfilling charitable objectives. It is an irrevocable split-interest trust that divides the beneficial interest between the income beneficiary (the client) and the charitable remainder beneficiary (S004, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/1.664-2, n.d.).

### 5.2 Key Mechanics

The trust is funded with $16,000,000.00 and provides the client with a fixed annual annuity of $800,000.00 at a payout rate of 5.00% (S004, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/1.664-2, n.d.). Over the full term the total annuity payments to the client equal $16,000,000.00 (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.).

Unlike a GRAT, the CRAT annuity is fixed at inception and does not vary with trust performance. The trust must distribute at least 5% of the initial fair market value annually, and the actuarial value of the charitable remainder must be at least 10% of the initial funding amount to qualify for the income tax deduction (S004, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/1.664-2, n.d.).

### 5.3 Tax Treatment

Upon establishment of the CRAT the client receives an upfront charitable income tax deduction of $7,620,722.64 under IRC Section 170 (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.). This deduction is based on the present value of the charity's remainder interest, calculated using IRS actuarial tables and the Section 7520 rate.

The trust corpus of $16,000,000.00 is removed from the taxable estate upon funding, reducing the taxable estate to $8,379,277.36 and saving an estimated $3,048,289.06 in estate taxes (S004, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/1.664-2, n.d.). Capital gains on appreciated assets transferred to the CRAT are deferred; the trust is generally exempt from income tax on gains reinvested within the trust (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.).

### 5.4 Advantages

- Charitable income tax deduction of $7,620,722.64 in the year of trust creation (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.)
- Complete removal of $16,000,000.00 from the taxable estate (S004, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/1.664-2, n.d.)
- Reliable income stream of $800,000.00 per year (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.)
- Capital gains deferral on appreciated assets (S004, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/1.664-2, n.d.)
- Philanthropic legacy: The remainder of $16,000,000.00 passes to the designated charity (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.)

### 5.5 Risks and Limitations

- No wealth passes to the client's children through the CRAT; the children receive $0.00 from this vehicle (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.)
- The annuity is fixed and does not adjust for inflation (S004, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/1.664-2, n.d.)
- If the trust's investment returns fall below the payout rate, the corpus will be depleted before term end, reducing or eliminating the charitable remainder (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.)
- The CRAT is irrevocable; the charitable beneficiary cannot be changed after funding (S004, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/1.664-2, n.d.)
- A separate strategy (e.g., a wealth replacement trust) would be needed to compensate heirs for the assets passing to charity (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.)

## 6. Comparative Analysis

The following table compares the two trust strategies on key planning dimensions (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.).

The GRAT transfers an estimated $3,705,126.78 to the next generation while still achieving meaningful estate tax reduction (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.). It is the superior vehicle for intergenerational wealth transfer but carries mortality risk during the trust term.

## 7. Scenario Illustration

### GRAT Scenario

The client funds a 10-year GRAT with $16,000,000.00 (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.). The Section 7520 hurdle rate is 196 bps. The trust is structured to pay an annual annuity of $1,777,498.67 back to the grantor each year (S004, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/1.664-2, n.d.).

Assuming the trust assets grow at 5.00% annually, the trust will have distributed $17,774,986.70 in total annuity payments over the term. The assets remaining after all annuity payments — $3,705,126.78 — pass to the two children free of gift and estate tax (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.).

The taxable gift at inception is only $0.00 because the GRAT was zeroed-out (S004, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/1.664-2, n.d.). The estate is reduced and tax savings of $1,482,050.71 are achieved.

### CRAT Scenario

Alternatively, the client funds a CRAT with $16,000,000.00 (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.). The trust pays a fixed annual annuity of $800,000.00 to the client for the trust term (S004, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/1.664-2, n.d.).

The client receives an upfront charitable income tax deduction of $7,620,722.64 under IRC Section 170 (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.). ### Net Effect Comparison

Under the GRAT scenario, the children receive $3,705,126.78 and the estate tax bill is reduced by $1,482,050.71 (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.). The scenarios are not mutually exclusive; a combined approach is discussed in the Recommendation section.

## 9. Key Risks and Considerations

### Legislative Risk

Congress has periodically considered legislation that would limit or eliminate zeroed-out GRATs, impose minimum trust terms, or change the estate tax exemption and rate structure. Any such changes could materially affect the projected outcomes described in this report (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.).

### Market Risk

Both strategies depend on investment returns. The GRAT's effectiveness requires returns exceeding the Section 7520 hurdle rate of 196 bps (S004, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/1.664-2, n.d.). The CRAT requires returns sufficient to fund the annuity payments while preserving the charitable remainder. Sustained market downturns could erode the projected benefits of either strategy (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.).

### Longevity Risk

The GRAT carries significant mortality risk: if the grantor dies during the 10-year term, the full corpus is included in the gross estate, eliminating the estate tax benefit (S004, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/1.664-2, n.d.). The CRAT does not carry this risk because the corpus is removed from the estate at funding (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.).

### Execution Complexity

Both trusts are irrevocable and require careful drafting by qualified estate counsel. The GRAT demands precise structuring of the annuity to satisfy Section 2702 requirements. The CRAT must meet the requirements of Section 664 and applicable Treasury regulations (S004, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/1.664-2, n.d.). Ongoing administration, annual filings, and investment management add complexity to both structures (S002, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/25.2702-3, n.d.).

### Interest Rate Sensitivity

The economics of both trusts are sensitive to the Section 7520 rate. A lower rate benefits the GRAT (smaller required annuity), while a higher rate benefits the CRAT (larger charitable deduction). The rate used in this analysis is 196 bps (S004, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/26/1.664-2, n.d.).
