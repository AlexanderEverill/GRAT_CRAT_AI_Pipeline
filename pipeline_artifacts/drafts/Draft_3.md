## §7520 Rate and Performance

Under IRC §671, the GRAT is treated as a grantor trust, meaning the grantor pays income tax on trust earnings during the term. This allows the trust corpus to grow without being diminished by taxes, effectively constituting an additional tax-free gift to beneficiaries.

The client, aged 62, must survive to age 72 for the GRAT to be effective. According to IRS actuarial tables, there is an approximate 15-20% mortality probability over this 10-year term [S006]. ## Term Selection Justification

The 10-year term is chosen to balance mortality risk and wealth transfer potential. Shorter rolling GRATs could reduce mortality risk but may not maximize wealth transfer due to frequent resets. Conversely, longer terms increase mortality risk, potentially negating estate tax benefits.

## Zeroed-Out Structure

The GRAT is structured to produce a $0 taxable gift, known as a "zeroed-out" GRAT. This is achieved by setting the annuity payments to match the initial corpus value, ensuring no immediate gift tax liability [S002].

## CRAT Analysis

# CRAT Analysis

## CRAT Structures

A Charitable Remainder Annuity Trust (CRAT) can be structured as either a term-of-years or a lifetime trust. The term-of-years CRAT is limited to a maximum of 20 years under 26 CFR §1.664-2 [S004]. If the client opts for a term-of-years CRAT and passes away before the term ends, the annuity payments cease, and the remaining corpus is transferred to the designated charity. In contrast, a lifetime CRAT continues to pay the annuity until the donor's death, at which point the remainder passes to the charity.

## Charitable Remainder Minimum

The CRAT must meet a 10% charitable remainder minimum, meaning the present value of the remainder interest must be at least 10% of the initial trust corpus [S004]. For this client, the CRAT clears this threshold, ensuring compliance with regulatory requirements.

## Taxation of Distributions

CRAT distributions are subject to a four-tier taxation system under IRC §664. Distributions are taxed in the following order: (1) ordinary income, (2) capital gains, (3) other income, and (4) return of corpus [S003]. This structure allows for tax-efficient distribution planning, as the trust itself is generally exempt from income tax.

## Estate Tax Treatment

Under IRC §2036, the retained annuity interest in a CRAT is included in the gross estate. However, an offsetting charitable deduction is available, which reduces the net estate tax liability [S006]. This differs from a GRAT, where the entire corpus is included in the estate without an offsetting deduction.

## Performance Dependence

The CRAT's performance is dependent on the payout rate, which is set at 5.00% for this client. The trust must generate returns exceeding this rate to preserve the corpus for the charity. In contrast, a GRAT's performance is measured against the §7520 rate, currently 1.96% (196 bps) [S007]. The GRAT is less sensitive to underperformance due to this lower hurdle rate, making it potentially more favorable in low-interest environments.

## Mortality Risk

Unlike a GRAT, a CRAT carries no mortality risk equivalent. If the grantor dies during the term, the annuity payments simply cease, and the remainder passes to the charity without estate inclusion penalties. This feature aligns with the client's goal of supporting charitable giving while minimizing estate tax exposure.

## Conclusion

For this client, the CRAT offers a structured approach to philanthropy with significant tax advantages, including a charitable deduction of $7,620,722.64 and estate tax savings of $3,048,289.06. However, it does not facilitate wealth transfer to heirs, as the remainder is designated for charity. Balancing these factors, the CRAT serves as a complementary tool to the GRAT, which focuses on intergenerational wealth transfer.
