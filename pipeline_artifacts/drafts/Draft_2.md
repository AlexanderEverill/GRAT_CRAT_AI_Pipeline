## CRAT Analysis

# CRAT Analysis

A Charitable Remainder Annuity Trust (CRAT) can be structured as either a term-of-years or a lifetime trust. The term-of-years CRAT is limited to a maximum of 20 years under 26 CFR §1.664-2 [S004]. If the client dies during this term, the annuity ceases, and the remaining corpus passes to charity without estate penalties. In a lifetime CRAT, annuity payments continue until death, with the remainder passing to charity at that time.

The CRAT must meet a 10% charitable remainder minimum, meaning the actuarial value of the remainder interest must be at least 10% of the initial corpus [S004]. This client's CRAT satisfies this requirement, ensuring compliance.

CRAT distributions are taxed in a four-tier system: ordinary income, capital gains, other income, and return of corpus [S003]. The trust itself is generally exempt from income tax.

Under IRC §2036, the retained annuity interest is included in the gross estate, but an offsetting charitable deduction reduces the net estate tax [S003]. This differs from a GRAT, where estate inclusion results in no offset.

The CRAT's performance depends on exceeding the payout rate of 5.00%, whereas the GRAT depends on surpassing the §7520 rate of 1.96% [S007]. The CRAT carries no mortality risk equivalent to the GRAT, making it a stable option for philanthropic goals without impacting wealth transfer to children.
