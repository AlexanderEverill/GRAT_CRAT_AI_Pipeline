## GRAT Analysis

A Grantor Retained Annuity Trust (GRAT) is a financial instrument designed to transfer wealth while minimizing estate and gift taxes. It involves the grantor transferring assets into an irrevocable trust, receiving fixed annuity payments for a specified term, with the remainder passing to beneficiaries, typically children, at the end of the term [S001].

## Funding and Transfer-Tax Implications

The GRAT is funded with assets such as cash or appreciated securities. The transfer is irrevocable, removing the assets from the grantor's estate. For transfer-tax purposes, the GRAT "freezes" the value of the transferred assets. The taxable gift is calculated as the present value of the remainder interest, not the full corpus, often resulting in a zeroed-out gift if structured correctly [S002].

Under IRC §671, the GRAT is treated as a grantor trust for income tax purposes. The grantor is responsible for paying taxes on the trust's income, allowing the trust assets to grow unencumbered by income tax liabilities [S001].

## CRAT Analysis
