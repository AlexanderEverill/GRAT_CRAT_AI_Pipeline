## Executive Summary

### Client Profile

This report analyses two estate-planning strategies for a 62-year-old married client with two adult children who realised a $16,000,000.00 liquidity event from the sale of an advertising agency in 2015. The applicable federal estate tax rate is 40.00% on amounts above the combined marital exemption of $10.86 million (S010, IRS Estate Tax — Exemptions, Rates, and Filing Requirements, 2026).

### The §7520 Hurdle Rate

Both strategies are evaluated using the IRS Section 7520 rate of 196 bps — the discount rate prescribed by the IRS for valuing annuity, life-estate, and remainder interests in trusts (S007, IRC §7520 — Valuation Tables (Section 7520 Hurdle Rate), 2026). For a GRAT, this rate acts as a hurdle: the trust only transfers wealth to beneficiaries to the extent that actual investment returns exceed the §7520 rate. For a CRAT, the §7520 rate is used to calculate the present value of the charitable remainder, which determines the upfront income tax deduction (S007, IRC §7520 — Valuation Tables (Section 7520 Hurdle Rate), 2026).

### Key Findings

**Grantor Retained Annuity Trust (GRAT):** A 10-year zeroed-out GRAT would save an estimated $1,482,050.71 in estate taxes and transfer $3,705,126.78 to the client's children (S001, IRC §2702 — Special Valuation Rules (GRAT Core Statute), 2026). The GRAT is a grantor trust under IRC §671, meaning the grantor pays income tax on trust earnings — effectively a tax-free gift that further reduces the estate (S008, IRC §671 — Grantor Trust Income Tax Rules, 2026). However, the GRAT carries **mortality risk**: if the grantor (age 62) does not survive the 10-year term (i.e., to age 72), the full corpus is included in the gross estate under IRC §2033, nullifying the transfer benefit (S006, IRC §2033 — Property Included in Gross Estate, 2026).

**Charitable Remainder Annuity Trust (CRAT):** A 20-year CRAT would produce estate tax savings of $3,048,289.06 and generate an upfront charitable income tax deduction of $7,620,722.64 under IRC §170 (S009, IRC §170 — Charitable Contributions and Gifts (Income Tax Deduction), 2026). The CRAT does not transfer wealth to children ($0.00); the remainder passes to charity (S003, IRC §664 — Charitable Remainder Trusts (CRAT Core Statute), 2026). Unlike the GRAT, the CRAT carries no mortality risk because the corpus is removed from the estate at funding. However, the CRAT's effectiveness depends on investment returns exceeding the 5.00% payout rate to preserve the charitable remainder (S004, 26 CFR §1.664-2 — Charitable Remainder Annuity Trust Regulations, 2026).

### Recommendation

Given the client's dual priorities of benefiting children and supporting charity, the GRAT is recommended as the **primary** strategy for its unique ability to transfer wealth to the next generation. The CRAT serves as a **complementary** vehicle for the client's philanthropic objectives and provides superior estate tax savings. A combined approach maximises overall planning benefit.

### Report Scope

This analysis is limited to the federal estate, gift, and income tax implications of the two trust structures. State-level taxes, alternative planning vehicles (e.g., dynasty trusts, family limited partnerships), and post-implementation investment management are outside the scope of this report.

## GRAT Analysis

### Mechanics of a GRAT under IRC §2702

A Grantor Retained Annuity Trust is established under IRC §2702, which requires the grantor to retain a qualified annuity interest for a fixed term (S001, IRC §2702 — Special Valuation Rules (GRAT Core Statute), 2026). The trust is funded with $16,000,000.00 from the client's liquidity event proceeds. The present value of the retained annuity interest is determined using IRS actuarial tables and the Section 7520 rate in effect at the time of the transfer (S002, 26 CFR §25.2702-3 — Qualified Interests (GRAT Regulations), 2026).

The GRAT pays a fixed annuity to the grantor for 10 years. At the end of the trust term, any remaining assets pass to the designated beneficiaries — in this case, the client's two adult children — free of additional gift or estate tax (S005, IRC §2501 — Imposition of Gift Tax, 2026).

### The §7520 Hurdle Rate and GRAT Performance Dependence

The Section 7520 rate of 196 bps functions as the **hurdle rate** for the GRAT (S007, IRC §7520 — Valuation Tables (Section 7520 Hurdle Rate), 2026). This IRS-prescribed discount rate is used to calculate the present value of the grantor's retained annuity stream. The GRAT transfers wealth to beneficiaries only to the extent that the trust's actual investment returns **exceed** this hurdle rate. If trust assets grow at exactly the §7520 rate, the remainder to beneficiaries is zero. If trust assets grow below the §7520 rate, the annuity payments will deplete the corpus and nothing passes to beneficiaries (S007, IRC §7520 — Valuation Tables (Section 7520 Hurdle Rate), 2026).

At the current §7520 rate of 196 bps, the assumed growth rate of 5.00% provides a meaningful spread above the hurdle rate, which is what generates the projected remainder to children (S007, IRC §7520 — Valuation Tables (Section 7520 Hurdle Rate), 2026). A low §7520 rate environment, such as the present one, is generally favourable for GRATs because the required annuity to zero out the gift is smaller, leaving more potential remainder for beneficiaries (S007, IRC §7520 — Valuation Tables (Section 7520 Hurdle Rate), 2026).

### Projected Outcomes

The annual annuity payment is $1,777,498.67 returned to the grantor each year (S002, 26 CFR §25.2702-3 — Qualified Interests (GRAT Regulations), 2026). Over the full 10-year term, total annuity payments equal $17,774,986.70. The annuity is structured as a "zeroed-out" GRAT where the present value of the annuity stream approximately equals the initial corpus, resulting in a taxable gift of $0.00 (S001, IRC §2702 — Special Valuation Rules (GRAT Core Statute), 2026).

Assuming trust assets grow at 5.00% annually (above the 196 bps hurdle rate), the projected remainder passing to children is $3,705,126.78 (S007, IRC §7520 — Valuation Tables (Section 7520 Hurdle Rate), 2026). The resulting taxable estate is reduced to $12,294,873.22, saving $1,482,050.71 in estate taxes at the 40.00% rate (S010, IRS Estate Tax — Exemptions, Rates, and Filing Requirements, 2026).

### Grantor Trust Income Tax Treatment (IRC §671)

The GRAT is a **grantor trust** for income tax purposes under IRC §671 (S008, IRC §671 — Grantor Trust Income Tax Rules, 2026). This means the grantor — not the trust — pays income tax on all trust earnings during the 10-year term. While this may appear to be a burden, it is actually a significant planning advantage: the grantor's income tax payments effectively constitute an additional tax-free gift to the remainder beneficiaries because the payments reduce the grantor's taxable estate while allowing the trust corpus to grow undiminished by income taxes (S008, IRC §671 — Grantor Trust Income Tax Rules, 2026). The trust assets compound at the full pre-tax rate, enhancing the projected remainder to children.

### Mortality Risk — The Critical GRAT Limitation

The GRAT's most significant risk is **mortality during the trust term**. Under IRC §2033, if the grantor dies before the end of the 10-year annuity term, the entire trust corpus is included in the grantor's gross estate (S006, IRC §2033 — Property Included in Gross Estate, 2026). This would completely nullify the intended estate tax savings and wealth transfer benefit.

For this client, age 62, a 10-year GRAT term requires survival to age 72. Based on IRS actuarial tables, a 62-year-old male has a life expectancy of approximately 21 additional years (to approximately age 83), making a 10-year term actuarially reasonable but not without meaningful risk (S006, IRC §2033 — Property Included in Gross Estate, 2026). The probability of dying during the term increases with the client's age, and any health impairment at the time of trust creation would elevate this risk substantially.

**Term selection rationale:** The 10-year term balances two competing considerations. A shorter term (e.g., 2–3 years) would reduce mortality risk but also reduce the potential wealth transfer because less time is available for growth above the hurdle rate. A longer term (e.g., 15–20 years) would increase the potential remainder but proportionally increase the probability of estate inclusion from death during the term. For a 62-year-old grantor, 10 years represents a moderate choice that provides meaningful transfer potential while keeping the mortality risk within acceptable bounds (S006, IRC §2033 — Property Included in Gross Estate, 2026).

### Gift Tax Implications

The taxable gift at inception is $0.00 because the present value of the retained annuity equals the corpus under the zeroed-out structure (S005, IRC §2501 — Imposition of Gift Tax, 2026). This minimises the gift tax cost of the transfer while allowing any growth above the §7520 hurdle rate to pass tax-free to beneficiaries (S001, IRC §2702 — Special Valuation Rules (GRAT Core Statute), 2026).

### Additional Risks

- If trust assets grow below the 196 bps Section 7520 rate, the remainder to beneficiaries will be reduced or eliminated (S007, IRC §7520 — Valuation Tables (Section 7520 Hurdle Rate), 2026)
- Legislative risk: Congress has periodically considered imposing minimum GRAT terms or eliminating zeroed-out GRATs (S001, IRC §2702 — Special Valuation Rules (GRAT Core Statute), 2026)
- The GRAT does not generate a charitable deduction; clients with strong philanthropic objectives need a complementary vehicle (S002, 26 CFR §25.2702-3 — Qualified Interests (GRAT Regulations), 2026)

## CRAT Analysis

### CRAT Mechanics under IRC §664

A Charitable Remainder Annuity Trust is governed by IRC §664, which establishes the requirements for split-interest trusts that provide a fixed annuity to the income beneficiary with the remainder passing to a qualified charity (S003, IRC §664 — Charitable Remainder Trusts (CRAT Core Statute), 2026). The CRAT is irrevocable: once funded, neither the corpus nor the charitable beneficiary can be changed (S003, IRC §664 — Charitable Remainder Trusts (CRAT Core Statute), 2026).

### Term Structure: Term-of-Years versus Lifetime

A CRAT may be structured for either (a) the lifetime of the income beneficiary or (b) a fixed term of years, subject to a **maximum of 20 years** under the regulations (S004, 26 CFR §1.664-2 — Charitable Remainder Annuity Trust Regulations, 2026). This analysis uses a **20-year term-of-years** CRAT, which is the maximum permissible fixed term.

The choice between a lifetime and a term-of-years CRAT involves important trade-offs. A **lifetime CRAT** would continue annuity payments until the client's death, providing income security regardless of longevity. For a 62-year-old client, this could mean payments continuing for 20+ years based on actuarial life expectancy. However, a lifetime CRAT generally produces a smaller charitable deduction under IRC §170 because the remainder interest is reduced by the longer expected payout period (S009, IRC §170 — Charitable Contributions and Gifts (Income Tax Deduction), 2026).

A **term-of-years CRAT** (as modelled here at 20 years) provides certainty of the payout duration and generally produces a larger charitable deduction because the charity is guaranteed to receive the remainder after a fixed period (S003, IRC §664 — Charitable Remainder Trusts (CRAT Core Statute), 2026). The trade-off is that annuity payments cease after 20 years regardless of whether the client is still living.

### The 10% Charitable Remainder Minimum

Under the regulations at 26 CFR §1.664-2, the actuarial value of the charitable remainder interest must be **at least 10%** of the initial net fair market value of the trust corpus at the time of funding (S004, 26 CFR §1.664-2 — Charitable Remainder Annuity Trust Regulations, 2026). If the combination of payout rate, trust term, and §7520 rate produces a remainder worth less than 10%, the trust fails to qualify as a CRAT and the charitable income tax deduction is disallowed. In this analysis, the charitable deduction of $7,620,722.64 relative to the $16,000,000.00 corpus well exceeds the 10% minimum threshold (S003, IRC §664 — Charitable Remainder Trusts (CRAT Core Statute), 2026).

### Key Mechanics

The trust is funded with $16,000,000.00 and provides a fixed annual annuity of $800,000.00 at a payout rate of 5.00% (S004, 26 CFR §1.664-2 — Charitable Remainder Annuity Trust Regulations, 2026). Unlike a CRUT (Charitable Remainder Unitrust), the CRAT annuity is **fixed at inception** and does not vary with trust performance. Over the full 20-year term, total annuity payments to the client equal $16,000,000.00 (S003, IRC §664 — Charitable Remainder Trusts (CRAT Core Statute), 2026).

### CRAT Performance Dependence

The CRAT's effectiveness depends on investment returns exceeding the 5.00% payout rate (S004, 26 CFR §1.664-2 — Charitable Remainder Annuity Trust Regulations, 2026). Unlike the GRAT, where the §7520 hurdle rate determines transfer efficiency, the CRAT's critical benchmark is the payout rate itself. If the trust earns less than 5.00% annually, the corpus erodes over time because the fixed annuity depletes principal. Sustained underperformance can exhaust the trust corpus before the 20-year term ends, reducing or completely eliminating the charitable remainder (S004, 26 CFR §1.664-2 — Charitable Remainder Annuity Trust Regulations, 2026). If the corpus is exhausted, the trust terminates early and the charity receives nothing.

In contrast, the GRAT's success depends on returns exceeding the lower §7520 hurdle rate (196 bps), making its performance threshold generally more favourable in a low-rate environment (S007, IRC §7520 — Valuation Tables (Section 7520 Hurdle Rate), 2026).

### Tax Treatment and Four-Tier Taxation of Distributions

Upon establishment of the CRAT, the client receives an upfront charitable income tax deduction of $7,620,722.64 under IRC §170 (S009, IRC §170 — Charitable Contributions and Gifts (Income Tax Deduction), 2026). This deduction is based on the present value of the charity's remainder interest, calculated using IRS actuarial tables and the §7520 rate of 196 bps (S007, IRC §7520 — Valuation Tables (Section 7520 Hurdle Rate), 2026).

Annuity distributions to the income beneficiary are taxed under the **four-tier system** prescribed by IRC §664 (S003, IRC §664 — Charitable Remainder Trusts (CRAT Core Statute), 2026):

1. **Ordinary income** — distributions are first characterised as ordinary income to the extent of the trust's current and accumulated ordinary income
2. **Capital gains** — next as capital gains to the extent of the trust's current and accumulated capital gains
3. **Other income** — then as tax-exempt income and other categories
4. **Return of corpus** — finally as a tax-free return of the donor's original contribution

The trust itself is generally exempt from income tax on reinvested gains, but the character of distributions flows through to the beneficiary (S003, IRC §664 — Charitable Remainder Trusts (CRAT Core Statute), 2026).

### Estate Tax Treatment under IRC §2036

The CRAT's estate tax treatment differs fundamentally from the GRAT. The grantor's retained annuity interest in the CRAT is an IRC §2036 retained interest, meaning it is included in the gross estate at death (S006, IRC §2033 — Property Included in Gross Estate, 2026). However, the estate receives an offsetting charitable deduction for the present value of the charitable remainder interest, which is what produces the net estate tax reduction. The estate tax savings of $3,048,289.06 reflect the charitable deduction's effect on the taxable estate, reducing it to $8,379,277.36 (S010, IRS Estate Tax — Exemptions, Rates, and Filing Requirements, 2026).

### Advantages

- Charitable income tax deduction of $7,620,722.64 in the year of trust creation (S009, IRC §170 — Charitable Contributions and Gifts (Income Tax Deduction), 2026)
- No mortality risk for estate inclusion — unlike the GRAT, the grantor's death during the term does not cause the full corpus to be included in the estate (S003, IRC §664 — Charitable Remainder Trusts (CRAT Core Statute), 2026)
- Reliable fixed income stream of $800,000.00 per year for 20 years (S004, 26 CFR §1.664-2 — Charitable Remainder Annuity Trust Regulations, 2026)
- Philanthropic legacy: $16,000,000.00 projected remainder to the designated charity (S003, IRC §664 — Charitable Remainder Trusts (CRAT Core Statute), 2026)
- Capital gains deferral on appreciated assets contributed to the trust (S003, IRC §664 — Charitable Remainder Trusts (CRAT Core Statute), 2026)

### Risks and Limitations

- No wealth passes to the client's children through the CRAT ($0.00); a separate wealth replacement trust (e.g., an ILIT funded with CRAT annuity payments) would be needed to compensate heirs (S003, IRC §664 — Charitable Remainder Trusts (CRAT Core Statute), 2026)
- The fixed annuity does not adjust for inflation — the real value of $800,000.00 per year erodes over a 20-year period (S004, 26 CFR §1.664-2 — Charitable Remainder Annuity Trust Regulations, 2026)
- If investment returns fall below the 5.00% payout rate, the corpus will be depleted, reducing or eliminating the charitable remainder (S004, 26 CFR §1.664-2 — Charitable Remainder Annuity Trust Regulations, 2026)
- The trust is irrevocable; neither the terms nor the charitable beneficiary can be changed after funding (S003, IRC §664 — Charitable Remainder Trusts (CRAT Core Statute), 2026)
- The maximum term-of-years is limited to 20 years; a longer income stream requires a lifetime CRAT structure (S004, 26 CFR §1.664-2 — Charitable Remainder Annuity Trust Regulations, 2026)

### Performance Dependence Comparison

The GRAT and CRAT have fundamentally different performance benchmarks. The GRAT transfers wealth only to the extent that investment returns **exceed the §7520 hurdle rate** of 196 bps (S007, IRC §7520 — Valuation Tables (Section 7520 Hurdle Rate), 2026). At the assumed growth rate of 5.00%, the spread above the hurdle rate generates the projected remainder of $3,705,126.78 to children (S001, IRC §2702 — Special Valuation Rules (GRAT Core Statute), 2026). If returns merely equal the §7520 rate, the remainder is zero.

The CRAT's critical benchmark is the **payout rate** of 5.00% (S004, 26 CFR §1.664-2 — Charitable Remainder Annuity Trust Regulations, 2026). Returns must equal or exceed this rate to preserve the corpus over the 20-year term. If returns fall below the payout rate, the corpus erodes and the charitable remainder is diminished (S004, 26 CFR §1.664-2 — Charitable Remainder Annuity Trust Regulations, 2026). The GRAT's hurdle rate (196 bps) is substantially lower than the CRAT's payout rate (5.00%), making the GRAT less sensitive to investment performance in the current low-rate environment (S007, IRC §7520 — Valuation Tables (Section 7520 Hurdle Rate), 2026).

### Mortality-Risk Analysis

For a 62-year-old grantor with a 10-year GRAT term, mortality risk is a material consideration (S006, IRC §2033 — Property Included in Gross Estate, 2026). Death during the GRAT term causes the full corpus to be included in the gross estate under IRC §2033, completely negating the estate tax benefit (S006, IRC §2033 — Property Included in Gross Estate, 2026). The CRAT does not carry this binary risk — the estate receives a charitable deduction regardless of when the grantor dies.

### Estate Tax Comparison

The CRAT provides greater **gross** estate tax savings ($3,048,289.06 vs $1,482,050.71) because the charitable deduction reduces the taxable estate by $7,620,722.64 (S009, IRC §170 — Charitable Contributions and Gifts (Income Tax Deduction), 2026). However, the CRAT achieves this by directing the remainder to charity rather than to children. The GRAT is the only vehicle that transfers wealth to the next generation ($3,705,126.78 to children) while also providing estate tax reduction (S001, IRC §2702 — Special Valuation Rules (GRAT Core Statute), 2026).

### Recommendation

Given that the client has identified **both** children's inheritance and charitable giving as priorities, the GRAT is recommended as the **primary** estate-planning instrument because it is the only vehicle that achieves intergenerational wealth transfer (S001, IRC §2702 — Special Valuation Rules (GRAT Core Statute), 2026). The GRAT will save an estimated $1,482,050.71 in estate taxes and transfer $3,705,126.78 to the children at the 40.00% estate tax rate (S010, IRS Estate Tax — Exemptions, Rates, and Filing Requirements, 2026).

The CRAT is recommended as a **complementary** strategy to address the client's philanthropic objectives. It provides $3,048,289.06 in estate tax savings and a charitable income tax deduction of $7,620,722.64 (S009, IRC §170 — Charitable Contributions and Gifts (Income Tax Deduction), 2026), while directing $16,000,000.00 to the client's chosen charity (S003, IRC §664 — Charitable Remainder Trusts (CRAT Core Statute), 2026).

### Implementation Conditions

- If intergenerational wealth transfer is the **dominant** priority, a GRAT-only strategy maximises assets passing to the children (S001, IRC §2702 — Special Valuation Rules (GRAT Core Statute), 2026)
- If the charitable objective is dominant, a larger CRAT allocation increases total tax savings and charitable impact (S003, IRC §664 — Charitable Remainder Trusts (CRAT Core Statute), 2026)
- For the combined approach, the specific allocation between GRAT and CRAT should be calibrated to the client's relative weighting of children vs. charity goals
- The recommendation assumes current tax law remains in effect; material changes to estate, gift, or income tax rates should trigger re-evaluation (S010, IRS Estate Tax — Exemptions, Rates, and Filing Requirements, 2026)
