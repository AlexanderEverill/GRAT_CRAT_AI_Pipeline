## GRAT Analysis

A Grantor Retained Annuity Trust (GRAT) is an estate planning tool designed to minimize estate taxes by transferring future appreciation of assets to beneficiaries. The grantor transfers assets into the trust, retaining the right to receive annuity payments for a specified term. After the term, any remaining assets pass to the beneficiaries, typically children, free of additional gift tax [S001].

## Funding and Transfer-Tax Implications

The GRAT is funded with assets such as cash or appreciated securities. The transfer is irrevocable, permanently removing the assets from the grantor's estate. For transfer-tax purposes, the value of the taxable gift is the remainder interest, not the full corpus. This is because the annuity payments effectively "zero out" the gift value, meaning the taxable gift can be minimized or even eliminated [S002].

## §7520 Hurdle Rate and Performance

The §7520 rate, currently 1.96% (196 bps), serves as the hurdle rate for the GRAT. The trust must achieve a growth rate exceeding this rate to transfer wealth effectively. ## Income Tax Treatment

Under IRC §671, the GRAT is treated as a grantor trust for income tax purposes. This means the grantor is responsible for paying income taxes on the trust's earnings, allowing the trust assets to grow without tax erosion [S001].

## CRAT Analysis

A Charitable Remainder Annuity Trust (CRAT) is a trust structure that provides a fixed annuity to one or more noncharitable beneficiaries, with the remainder passing to a designated charity. The annuity is determined at the trust's inception based on the initial fair market value of the assets and remains constant regardless of the trust's investment performance [S004].

## Structure and Funding

CRATs can be structured for the lifetime of the beneficiary or for a term of years, with a maximum term of 20 years as per 26 CFR §1.664-2 [S004]. The trust is funded with assets that are irrevocably transferred, removing them from the donor's estate. For this client, the CRAT is funded with $16,000,000, and the annual annuity is set at $800,000, representing a 5.00% payout rate [S004].

The CRAT must meet a 10% charitable remainder minimum, ensuring that the present value of the remainder interest passing to charity is at least 10% of the initial fair market value [S004]. The estate tax savings from the CRAT are estimated at $3,048,289.06, due to the offsetting charitable deduction under IRC §2036 [S003].

Distributions from a CRAT are taxed under a four-tier system as outlined in IRC §664. The tiers include ordinary income, capital gains, other income, and return of principal, taxed in that order [S003]. This structure affects the tax efficiency of the annuity payments received by the noncharitable beneficiaries.

## Performance Dependence
